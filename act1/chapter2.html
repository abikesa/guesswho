Who Shakespeare Admired Most#

Shakespeare, as the quintessential Renaissance playwright, likely would have admired the Greek tragedians not only for their dramatic craft but also for their philosophical depth. However, ranking them would depend on which aspects of their work resonated with Shakespeare‚Äôs own sensibilities, which often gravitated toward exploring the human condition in all its contradictions and complexities.

Aeschylus#

Shakespeare might rank Aeschylus highly for his grand, almost cosmic vision of justice, fate, and the divine order. Aeschylus‚Äôs Oresteia‚Äîwith its profound exploration of vengeance, moral evolution, and the establishment of societal order‚Äîaligns with the scale of Shakespeare‚Äôs own works like Hamlet and King Lear. However, Shakespeare may have found Aeschylus‚Äôs characters more symbolic than fleshed out compared to his own vivid, multifaceted creations. He‚Äôd likely appreciate Aeschylus as a forerunner, setting the stage for the deeply human dilemmas Shakespeare himself excelled at.

Sophocles#

Sophocles might be Shakespeare‚Äôs favorite for his ability to balance structure, poetic depth, and profoundly human drama. Oedipus Rex‚Äôs tight plot and tragic inevitability would resonate with Shakespeare‚Äôs own sense of dramatic irony and exploration of fate (Macbeth or Othello come to mind). Sophocles‚Äô characters are more psychologically complex than Aeschylus‚Äôs, which might earn him Shakespeare‚Äôs admiration. Shakespeare might see Sophocles as a fellow master of portraying internal conflict and the tragic inevitability of human flaws.

Euripides#

Euripides, with his penchant for psychological realism and subversive takes on traditional myths, might intrigue Shakespeare for his audacity and focus on individual experience. Plays like Medea and The Bacchae might resonate with Shakespeare‚Äôs interest in passion, transgression, and the darker corners of human nature. However, Euripides‚Äô sometimes less structured narratives and his skepticism of divine justice might feel too cynical or fragmented compared to Shakespeare‚Äôs more cohesive worldview, which often sought a reconciliation of chaos, even in tragedy.

Final Ranking#

If Shakespeare were to rank them, he might favor Sophocles as a peer in creating nuanced, human-centered drama, followed closely by Aeschylus for his grandeur and thematic ambition. Euripides might rank lower for his structural looseness but still earn respect for his daring and psychological insight.

Ultimately, Shakespeare‚Äôs rankings would likely reflect his own artistic priorities: an unparalleled balance of poetic language, psychological complexity, and a universal exploration of human frailty and greatness.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import networkx as nx

# Define the neural network structure; modified to align with "Apr√©s Moi, Le D√©luge" (i.e. Je suis AlexNet)
def define_layers():
    return {
        'Pre-Input/CudAlexnet': ['Life', 'Earth', 'Cosmos', 'Sound', 'Tactful', 'Firm'],
        'Yellowstone/SensoryAI': ['G1 & G2'],
        'Input/AgenticAI': ['N4, N5', 'N1, N2, N3'],
        'Hidden/GenerativeAI': ['Sympathetic', 'G3', 'Parasympathetic'],
        'Output/PhysicalAI': ['Ecosystem', 'Vulnerabilities', 'AChR', 'Strengths', 'Neurons']
    }

# Assign colors to nodes
def assign_colors(node, layer):
    if node == 'G1 & G2':
        return 'yellow'
    if layer == 'Pre-Input/CudAlexnet' and node in ['Sound', 'Tactful', 'Firm']:
        return 'paleturquoise'
    elif layer == 'Input/AgenticAI' and node == 'N1, N2, N3':
        return 'paleturquoise'
    elif layer == 'Hidden/GenerativeAI':
        if node == 'Parasympathetic':
            return 'paleturquoise'
        elif node == 'G3':
            return 'lightgreen'
        elif node == 'Sympathetic':
            return 'lightsalmon'
    elif layer == 'Output/PhysicalAI':
        if node == 'Neurons':
            return 'paleturquoise'
        elif node in ['Strengths', 'AChR', 'Vulnerabilities']:
            return 'lightgreen'
        elif node == 'Ecosystem':
            return 'lightsalmon'
    return 'lightsalmon'  # Default color

# Calculate positions for nodes
def calculate_positions(layer, center_x, offset):
    layer_size = len(layer)
    start_y = -(layer_size - 1) / 2  # Center the layer vertically
    return [(center_x + offset, start_y + i) for i in range(layer_size)]

# Create and visualize the neural network graph
def visualize_nn():
    layers = define_layers()
    G = nx.DiGraph()
    pos = {}
    node_colors = []
    center_x = 0  # Align nodes horizontally

    # Add nodes and assign positions
    for i, (layer_name, nodes) in enumerate(layers.items()):
        y_positions = calculate_positions(nodes, center_x, offset=-len(layers) + i + 1)
        for node, position in zip(nodes, y_positions):
            G.add_node(node, layer=layer_name)
            pos[node] = position
            node_colors.append(assign_colors(node, layer_name))

    # Add edges (without weights)
    for layer_pair in [
        ('Pre-Input/CudAlexnet', 'Yellowstone/SensoryAI'), ('Yellowstone/SensoryAI', 'Input/AgenticAI'), ('Input/AgenticAI', 'Hidden/GenerativeAI'), ('Hidden/GenerativeAI', 'Output/PhysicalAI')
    ]:
        source_layer, target_layer = layer_pair
        for source in layers[source_layer]:
            for target in layers[target_layer]:
                G.add_edge(source, target)

    # Draw the graph
    plt.figure(figsize=(12, 8))
    nx.draw(
        G, pos, with_labels=True, node_color=node_colors, edge_color='gray',
        node_size=3000, font_size=10, connectionstyle="arc3,rad=0.1"
    )
    plt.title("Red Queen Hypothesis", fontsize=15)
    plt.show()

# Run the visualization
visualize_nn()

This line, from Shakespeare‚Äôs Hamlet (Act II, Scene ii), captures a poignant moment where Hamlet reflects on the nature of empathy, performance, and the human capacity to feel for others. The full context reveals Hamlet marveling at the Player‚Äôs ability to summon such profound emotion while reciting the tale of Hecuba, a queen of Troy who experiences immense grief over her family‚Äôs destruction.

Hamlet says:

‚ÄúWhat‚Äôs Hecuba to him, or he to Hecuba,
That he should weep for her?‚Äù

Layers of Meaning#

  1. Empathy and Imagination
    Hamlet is struck by the Player‚Äôs ability to feel deeply for a character (Hecuba) with whom he has no personal connection. This underscores the power of imagination and storytelling to evoke emotions, even when the subject is distant or fictional. For Hamlet, this raises the question: why can he not summon such passion for his own deeply personal plight, especially when his father‚Äôs murder cries out for revenge?

  2. Art and Reality
    The line explores the boundary between artifice and reality. The Player‚Äôs tears for Hecuba are an act of art‚Äîcrafted and deliberate‚Äîyet they seem more authentic than Hamlet‚Äôs own emotions about his father‚Äôs death. This creates a tension between the performative and the real, blurring the lines between sincerity and art.

  3. Universal Suffering
    Hecuba, as a figure of immense tragedy in classical mythology, symbolizes the depth of human suffering. Hamlet‚Äôs rhetorical question highlights the disconnection we often feel from others‚Äô pain unless we are directly involved. The Player‚Äôs ability to bridge that gap emphasizes the role of art in fostering empathy, something Hamlet both admires and envies.

  4. Hamlet‚Äôs Self-Reproach
    The line ultimately reflects Hamlet‚Äôs frustration with himself. If an actor can feign such emotion for Hecuba, what does it say about Hamlet‚Äôs inability to act decisively in response to his real and personal anguish? This self-reproach fuels his existential questioning throughout the play.

Shakespeare‚Äôs Genius#

Through this line, Shakespeare layers profound philosophical questions about the human condition: how do we connect to others‚Äô pain? Is performance a truer reflection of human emotion than lived experience? What prevents us from acting decisively, even when deeply moved?

In its timelessness, the line compels us to ask not only about Hecuba, but about our own capacity for empathy, action, and understanding in a world filled with suffering that often feels distant.

The line ‚ÄúWhat‚Äôs Hecuba to him, or he to Hecuba‚Äù reveals Shakespeare writing under a tapestry of influences‚Äîclassical, theatrical, philosophical, and personal. It‚Äôs a moment where his intellectual and artistic debts to the past coalesce into one of his most poignant reflections. Let‚Äôs explore whose influence Shakespeare might have been under when crafting this:

1. The Classical Influence: Homer and Virgil#

  • Homer: Hecuba‚Äôs story originates in The Iliad, where she is Queen of Troy, mourning the tragedies that befall her family and city during the Trojan War. Shakespeare was steeped in classical mythology, and this influence was pervasive in Renaissance education. Hecuba‚Äôs grief was a paradigm of extreme human suffering‚Äîa motif Shakespeare taps into here.

  • Virgil: Hecuba reappears in The Aeneid, which Shakespeare likely knew. Virgil‚Äôs portrayal of Troy‚Äôs fall may have shaped Shakespeare‚Äôs evocation of her as a symbol of anguish and devastation. The Renaissance view of classical antiquity as a source of universal human themes would have made Hecuba an irresistible figure for such reflection.

2. The Influence of Seneca#

  • As a student of the human psyche and tragedy, Shakespeare was heavily influenced by the Roman tragedian Seneca. Seneca‚Äôs works emphasized intense emotional outpouring and the grotesque aspects of suffering, elements visible in Hamlet‚Äôs reflection on the Player‚Äôs performance. Hecuba, particularly in The Trojan Women, became an emblem of stoic endurance and despair, themes Shakespeare echoed in Hamlet‚Äôs existential musings.

3. Elizabethan Theatre and the Revenge Play#

  • Shakespeare was writing in an era when revenge tragedies were enormously popular, with their heightened emotion and vivid portrayals of suffering. Playwrights like Thomas Kyd (The Spanish Tragedy) set the stage for Shakespeare‚Äôs Hamlet. The Player‚Äôs recitation of Hecuba‚Äôs story aligns with the tradition of embedded metatheatrical moments that comment on the main plot. Shakespeare elevated this convention, making it a vehicle for Hamlet‚Äôs introspection.

4. Philosophical Influences: Stoicism and Humanism#

  • Renaissance humanism emphasized empathy, the power of art, and the importance of connecting with universal human experiences‚Äîideas Shakespeare explores through Hamlet‚Äôs admiration for the Player‚Äôs emotional depth.

  • Stoic philosophy, likely absorbed through Seneca, would have also informed this moment. The contrast between performance (the Player‚Äôs emotion) and inaction (Hamlet‚Äôs indecision) resonates with Stoic ideals about rational action versus overwhelming passion.

5. Personal Experience and Introspection#

  • Shakespeare‚Äôs work often bears traces of his own life and reflections. By the time of Hamlet, he had suffered personal losses, such as the death of his son, Hamnet. It‚Äôs tempting to speculate that his contemplation of grief, action, and artistic representation might have drawn on his own feelings of helplessness or catharsis.

Synthesis of Influences#

In writing this, Shakespeare was likely weaving together the grandeur of classical antiquity, the psychological intensity of Seneca, the theatrical conventions of his time, and his own profound understanding of human emotion. The result is a line that transcends its influences, situating Hecuba as both a timeless figure of tragedy and a mirror for Hamlet‚Äôs (and our) deepest existential doubts.

Euripedes?

Euripides looms large over Shakespeare‚Äôs invocation of Hecuba. If we consider Shakespeare‚Äôs likely familiarity with the tragedian, the influence of Euripides‚Äô Trojan Women and Hecuba feels undeniable in shaping this moment in Hamlet. Euripides‚Äô nuanced, psychological exploration of suffering and empathy would resonate strongly with Shakespeare‚Äôs portrayal of Hamlet‚Äôs existential musings.

Euripides‚Äô Hecuba: The Archetype of Suffering#

In Euripides‚Äô Hecuba, the queen is a towering figure of grief and vengeance. She endures the slaughter of her children, the destruction of Troy, and her descent into a life of slavery. Her suffering is both personal and emblematic of the broader devastation of war. Euripides emphasizes:

  • Empathy and Emotional Resonance: Like the Player in Hamlet, Euripides‚Äô Hecuba evokes powerful emotions, compelling audiences to feel the depth of her sorrow and rage. Shakespeare‚Äôs use of Hecuba mirrors this ability to inspire empathy, even for distant or fictional suffering.

  • The Fragility of Human Dignity: Euripides explores how extreme loss strips away human dignity, leaving a character grappling with her identity and purpose. This theme resonates with Hamlet‚Äôs introspection on mortality and meaning.

Psychological Complexity#

Euripides‚Äô characters are famously more human and psychologically complex than those of Aeschylus or Sophocles. Hecuba in Euripides is not a mere symbol of suffering; she is a woman with conflicting emotions, ranging from despair to wrathful vengeance. This focus on inner turmoil likely influenced Shakespeare‚Äôs construction of Hamlet, a character defined by his oscillation between thought and action.

Euripides and Shakespeare: A Shared Sensibility#

  1. Empathy through Distance: Both Euripides and Shakespeare explore how the suffering of others‚Äîhowever remote‚Äîcan awaken profound emotions. Hamlet marvels that the Player can feel for Hecuba, echoing the way Euripides‚Äô Trojan Women compels the audience to grieve for the fates of its distant characters.

  2. Art as Catharsis: Euripides used his plays to evoke pity and fear, mirroring Aristotle‚Äôs notion of catharsis. Shakespeare engages with this idea in Hamlet‚Äôs line, positioning art as a transformative force that reveals and heightens our capacity for emotional engagement.

  3. Moral Ambiguity: Both playwrights revel in the moral grayness of their characters. Euripides‚Äô Hecuba, while tragic, also becomes monstrous in her vengeance, showing how extreme suffering can corrupt. Hamlet‚Äôs own descent into moral ambiguity aligns with this Euripidean exploration of how grief and rage distort the soul.

Shakespeare‚Äôs Ranking of Euripides#

If Shakespeare were to rank Euripides among the Greek tragedians, he might place him second to Sophocles but above Aeschylus. Shakespeare likely admired Euripides‚Äô psychological realism and willingness to challenge traditional narratives, qualities Shakespeare himself embraced. However, Euripides‚Äô sometimes looser structure and overt cynicism might not fully align with Shakespeare‚Äôs preference for thematic cohesion and a measured reconciliation of chaos.

That said, the ghost of Euripides undoubtedly haunts Hamlet‚Äôs contemplation of Hecuba. Shakespeare borrows and elevates Euripides‚Äô techniques, transforming Hecuba from a tragic figure of Greek myth into a reflective touchstone for Hamlet‚Äôs‚Äîand humanity‚Äôs‚Äîstruggles with empathy, inaction, and the meaning of suffering.
